The Geometric Derivative

Inspired by 3Blue1Brown's Essence of Calculus

Part 1  ·  The derivative of x²

If x² is the area of a square… what happens when x grows by a tiny amount dx?

Think of x² not as "x times x" but as the area of a square with side length x.

Now nudge x by a tiny amount dx. The side grows from x to x + dx. Three new regions appear:

■ Right strip:   x · dx
■ Top strip:    x · dx
■ Corner:       (dx)²   ← negligible

Total new area:   d(x²) = 2x · dx + (dx)²

But (dx)² is infinitesimally small — the square of a tiny number. Drop it.

d(x²) = 2x · dx   ⟹   d(x²)/dx = 2x   ✓
Part 2  ·  The derivative of 1/x

A rectangle has constant area = 1, with width x and height 1/x. When x grows by dx… what must happen to 1/x?

A rectangle whose area is always 1. Width = x, height = 1/x.

When x grows by dx, the rectangle gets wider. A thin green strip of area appears on the right:

■ Area gained:   (1/x) · dx

But the area must stay at 1. So the height shrinks, creating a red strip of lost area along the top:

■ Area lost:   x · |d(1/x)|

These must cancel exactly:

(1/x) · dx + x · d(1/x) = 0

Solve for d(1/x):

d(1/x) = −dx / x²
d(1/x)/dx = −1/x²   ✓
The negative sign is geometric necessity — a wider rectangle with the same area must get shorter.
Part 3  ·  The Product Rule

If f(x) = sin(x) · x², think of it as the area of a rectangle with sides sin(x) and x². What happens when x nudges by dx?

Think of f(x) = sin(x) · x² as the area of a rectangle with width sin(x) and height x².

When x nudges by dx, both sides change independently. sin(x) changes by d(sin(x)) and x² changes by d(x²). This creates two new strips:

■ Top strip:     sin(x) · d(x²)
■ Right strip:   x² · d(sin(x))
■ Corner:       negligible

So the total change in area is:

df = sin(x) · d(x²) + x² · d(sin(x))

We already know d(x²) = 2x · dx and d(sin(x)) = cos(x) · dx. Substitute:

df/dx = sin(x) · 2x + x² · cos(x)   ✓

In general:   (fg)' = f · g' + g · f'
Part 4  ·  The Chain Rule

For f(g(x)), think of three number lines: x feeds into g, and g feeds into f. A nudge to x ripples through.

The chain rule is about cascading nudges. A tiny nudge dx on the x number line gets scaled by g'(x) to produce dg on the middle line.

Then that nudge gets scaled again by f'(g) to produce df on the output line.

The total scaling factor from input to output is just the product of the two individual scaling factors:

df/dx = f'(g(x)) · g'(x)

Each function in the chain multiplies the nudge by its own derivative. That's it — derivatives compose by multiplication.
The Deep Pattern

Every derivative rule is a geometric story about what happens when you nudge the input by a tiny dx.

x²  →  Area grows freely. Count the new strips.
1/x  →  Area is locked. Gain on one side forces loss on another.
Product rule  →  Both sides of a rectangle change. Two strips, one for each factor.
Chain rule  →  Nudges cascade through a pipeline, each function scaling the nudge by its derivative.

No limits. No epsilons. Just geometry and tiny quantities.
