> **Why this lesson exists:** Lagrange's theorem is one of the first deep structural results in group theory, establishing that the size of any subgroup must divide the size of the whole group. This constraint has far-reaching consequences. In AI alignment research, quotient structures (groups "modded out" by subgroups) appear when we factor out symmetries in neural network weight spaces. Normal subgroups identify which symmetries can be cleanly factored out, and this connects directly to how Singular Learning Theory analyzes the parameter-to-function map by collapsing equivalent weight configurations. Understanding cosets and normality is essential for the isomorphism theorems in Lesson 82.

## Part 1: Cosets

### Left Cosets

Let H be a subgroup of a group G, and let g be an element of G. The **left coset** of H containing g is:

gH = {g * h : h in H}

This is the set of all elements obtained by multiplying g on the left by every element of H.

**Example.** Let G = Z_6 and H = {0, 3} (the subgroup of order 2). The left cosets are:
- 0 + H = {0, 3}
- 1 + H = {1, 4}
- 2 + H = {2, 5}

These three cosets partition Z_6 into three disjoint sets of equal size.

**Example.** Let G = S_3 and H = {e, (1 2)}. The left cosets are:
- eH = {e, (1 2)}
- (1 3)H = {(1 3), (1 2 3)}
- (2 3)H = {(2 3), (1 3 2)}

Again, three cosets, each of size 2, partitioning all 6 elements of S_3.

### Right Cosets

The **right coset** of H containing g is:

Hg = {h * g : h in H}

For abelian groups, left and right cosets are always the same. For non-abelian groups, they may differ. When left and right cosets coincide for all g, the subgroup H is called **normal** -- a critically important concept we address below.

### Fundamental Properties of Cosets

**Theorem.** Let H be a subgroup of G. Then:

1. Every element of G belongs to exactly one left coset of H.
2. Two left cosets are either identical or disjoint.
3. All left cosets have the same cardinality (= |H|).
4. g_1 H = g_2 H if and only if g_1^{-1} g_2 is in H.

Property (1) and (2) together say that the left cosets of H form a **partition** of G. This is one of the most elegant structural facts in elementary group theory: any subgroup carves the group into equal-sized, non-overlapping slices.

**Proof sketch for (4):** g_1 H = g_2 H means g_1 h_1 = g_2 h_2 for some h_1, h_2 in H. Then g_1^{-1} g_2 = h_1 h_2^{-1}, which is in H since H is a subgroup.

### The Index of a Subgroup

The **index** of H in G, written [G : H], is the number of distinct left cosets of H in G. If G is finite:

[G : H] = |G| / |H|

This number is always a positive integer (or infinite for infinite groups with finite subgroups of infinite index).

## Part 2: Lagrange's Theorem

### Statement and Proof

**Lagrange's Theorem.** If G is a finite group and H is a subgroup of G, then |H| divides |G|. Moreover, |G| = |H| * [G : H].

**Proof.** The left cosets of H partition G into [G : H] disjoint subsets, each of size |H|. Since every element of G belongs to exactly one coset:

|G| = (number of cosets) * (size of each coset) = [G : H] * |H|

Therefore |H| divides |G|.

This proof is remarkable in its simplicity yet its consequences are profound. It provides one of the most powerful constraints on the structure of finite groups.

### Immediate Consequences

**Corollary 1: Order of an element divides order of the group.**
If g is in G, then |g| divides |G|. (The subgroup generated by g, <g>, has order |g|, and by Lagrange's theorem, |g| divides |G|.)

**Corollary 2: Groups of prime order are cyclic.**
If |G| = p where p is prime, then G has no proper non-trivial subgroups (since any subgroup order must divide p). Every non-identity element must therefore generate all of G, so G is cyclic and isomorphic to Z_p.

**Corollary 3: Fermat's Little Theorem.**
For any integer a not divisible by prime p, we have a^{p-1} = 1 (mod p). This follows from applying Lagrange's theorem to the multiplicative group Z_p* of order p-1.

**Corollary 4:** The index satisfies a "tower law": if K <= H <= G are subgroups, then [G : K] = [G : H] * [H : K].

### What Lagrange's Theorem Does NOT Say

Lagrange's theorem says that subgroup orders divide group order. It does NOT guarantee that for every divisor d of |G|, there exists a subgroup of order d. This **converse** is false in general:

- A_4 (the alternating group on 4 elements) has order 12. The number 6 divides 12, but A_4 has no subgroup of order 6.

However, partial converses exist. Cauchy's theorem guarantees a subgroup of order p for every prime p dividing |G|, and the Sylow theorems give much more detailed information about subgroups of prime-power order.

## Part 3: Normal Subgroups

### Definition

A subgroup N of G is **normal** (written N <| G) if for every g in G:

gN = Ng

Equivalently, N is normal if and only if gNg^{-1} = N for all g in G, meaning N is invariant under **conjugation** by every element of G.

Yet another equivalent condition: N is normal if and only if gng^{-1} is in N for every g in G and every n in N. (We do not need gng^{-1} = n; it just needs to land somewhere in N.)

### Why Normality Matters

Normal subgroups are precisely the subgroups for which the set of cosets G/N can be given a well-defined group structure. If N is not normal, attempting to multiply cosets (g_1 N)(g_2 N) = g_1 g_2 N fails because the result depends on coset representatives, not just the cosets themselves.

This is the gateway to **quotient groups**, which are fundamental to the isomorphism theorems in Lesson 82.

### Examples of Normal Subgroups

1. **Every subgroup of an abelian group is normal.** (Since gn = ng for all elements, gN = Ng automatically.)

2. **The kernel of any homomorphism is normal.** (We will prove this in Lesson 82, but it is the most important source of normal subgroups.)

3. **A_n is normal in S_n** for all n. (The alternating group, consisting of even permutations, has index 2 in S_n.)

4. **Any subgroup of index 2 is normal.** If [G : H] = 2, there are exactly two cosets: H itself and the complement G \ H. Since left cosets and right cosets both partition G into H and G \ H, we must have gH = Hg for all g.

5. **SL_n(F) is normal in GL_n(F).** The determinant map provides the homomorphism whose kernel is SL_n.

### Non-Examples

The subgroup H = {e, (1 2)} in S_3 is NOT normal. We can verify: (1 3)(1 2)(1 3)^{-1} = (1 3)(1 2)(1 3) = (2 3), which is not in H.

## Part 4: The Normalizer and Center

### The Normalizer

For any subgroup H of G, the **normalizer** of H in G is:

N_G(H) = {g in G : gHg^{-1} = H}

This is the largest subgroup of G in which H is normal. We always have H <= N_G(H) <= G, and:
- H is normal in G if and only if N_G(H) = G.
- H is always normal in N_G(H).

The normalizer measures "how close to being normal" a subgroup is.

### The Center

The **center** of a group G is:

Z(G) = {z in G : zg = gz for all g in G}

This is the set of elements that commute with everything. Key facts:
- Z(G) is always a normal subgroup of G.
- G is abelian if and only if Z(G) = G.
- Z(S_n) = {e} for n >= 3 (the symmetric groups have trivial center).

**Example:** Z(D_4) = {e, r^2}. Only the identity and the 180-degree rotation commute with all symmetries of the square.

**Example:** Z(GL_n(F)) consists of all scalar matrices aI_n where a is nonzero. These are the only matrices that commute with all invertible matrices.

### The Centralizer

The **centralizer** of an element g in G is:

C_G(g) = {x in G : xg = gx}

This is the set of all elements that commute with g. The center is the intersection of all centralizers: Z(G) = intersection of C_G(g) over all g in G.

## Part 5: Simple Groups

### Definition and Significance

A group G is **simple** if it has no normal subgroups other than {e} and G itself. Simple groups are the "atoms" of group theory -- the building blocks from which all finite groups are constructed.

**Examples of simple groups:**
- Z_p for any prime p. (A group of prime order has no proper non-trivial subgroups at all.)
- A_n for n >= 5. (Proving that A_5 is simple is a significant result; it is connected to the unsolvability of the quintic by radicals.)
- The **Monster group**, a simple group of order approximately 8 x 10^{53}, with deep connections to modular forms and string theory.

### The Classification Theorem

The classification of finite simple groups is one of the greatest achievements of 20th-century mathematics. Every finite simple group falls into one of the following categories:
1. Cyclic groups Z_p of prime order.
2. Alternating groups A_n for n >= 5.
3. Groups of Lie type (systematic families related to matrix groups over finite fields).
4. 26 sporadic groups that do not fit into infinite families.

The proof spans tens of thousands of pages across hundreds of papers. We mention it here to show the depth and richness of the theory whose surface we are barely scratching.

### The Jordan-Holder Theorem

Every finite group has a **composition series** -- a chain of subgroups:

{e} = G_0 <| G_1 <| G_2 <| ... <| G_n = G

where each quotient G_{i+1}/G_i is simple. The **Jordan-Holder theorem** states that while the series itself is not unique, the list of simple quotients (the **composition factors**) is unique up to reordering.

This is the group-theoretic analogue of unique prime factorization for integers.

## Part 6: Connection to AI Alignment

### Quotient Structures and Weight Space

When we study neural networks through the lens of SLT, we need to understand the map from parameters (weights) to functions. Multiple parameter values can correspond to the same function, and the symmetry group acts on the parameter space.

The quotient W/G (weight space modulo the symmetry group) represents the space of truly distinct network functions. Understanding whether this quotient has nice structure requires understanding normal subgroups and quotient groups.

For instance, in a network with hidden layer of size n, the symmetric group S_n acts on the weight space. The orbits of this action (the cosets of the stabilizer subgroups) correspond to equivalence classes of weights computing the same function. The size of each orbit (predicted by the orbit-stabilizer theorem, Lesson 84) tells us how many copies of each function exist in weight space.

### Sign Symmetries

Beyond permutation symmetries, neural networks with odd activation functions (like tanh) have additional sign-flip symmetries: negating all incoming and outgoing weights of a neuron preserves the function. These generate a group (Z_2)^n, and the full symmetry group is a semidirect product S_n extended by (Z_2)^n. Analyzing this combined symmetry requires all the tools of this lesson: cosets, normality, and the interplay between subgroups.

2. List all left cosets of H = {e, (1 2)} in S_3. Then list all right cosets. Are they the same? What does this tell you about the normality of H?

3. Find all normal subgroups of D_4. Draw the subgroup lattice and indicate which subgroups are normal.

4. Compute the center Z(D_6) (the dihedral group of the regular hexagon).

5. Find the composition series of S_4 and identify each composition factor.

### Proof-Based

6. Prove that if [G : H] = 2, then H is normal in G.

7. Prove that the intersection of two normal subgroups is normal.

8. Prove that Z(G) is a normal subgroup of G.

9. Prove that if G/Z(G) is cyclic, then G is abelian. (Hint: this means every element can be written as z * g^k for some z in Z(G).)

10. Prove Cauchy's theorem for abelian groups: if G is a finite abelian group and p is a prime dividing |G|, then G has an element of order p.

### Conceptual

11. Lagrange's theorem gives a necessary condition for a number to be the order of a subgroup. Give an example showing this condition is not sufficient (find a group with no subgroup of some order that divides the group order).

12. Explain intuitively why subgroups of index 2 are always normal, using the coset partition picture.

13. Consider a neural network with one hidden layer of 4 neurons and ReLU activation. Describe the symmetry group. How many elements does it have? What are the cosets of the stabilizer of a generic weight configuration?

14. The group Q_8 = {1, -1, i, -i, j, -j, k, -k} (the quaternion group) has the property that every subgroup is normal. Verify this by listing all subgroups and checking normality. What is special about such groups (called Dedekind groups)?
